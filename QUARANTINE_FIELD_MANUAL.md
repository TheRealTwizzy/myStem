## Errata on File

**ERR-01** — Issued 2087-10-28
*Affects: Section 3.4 (Thermal Response Protocols, Revision 1)*

Previous guidance stated: *"Specimens can be reliably deterred by sustained heat sources directed at the enclosure boundary."*

This guidance is **RESCINDED**. Field reports from four independent sites confirm that specimens exposed to directed sustained heat are developing accelerated Dermal Spread adaptations. The heat is being used, not avoided. In two documented cases, specimens that were targeted with thermal deterrents reached Settled Form faster than untreated specimens in equivalent environments.

Applying heat to deter Dermal Spread-pathway specimens is equivalent to providing supplemental environmental pressure for Dermal Spread. Do not do this.

See Section 3.4-R (this revision) for replacement guidance.

---

**ERR-02** — Issued 2088-01-17
*Affects: Section 5.1 (Isolation Protocol, Revision 1)*

Previous guidance stated: *"Sealed isolation chambers prevent environmental stimulus and halt developmental progression."*

This guidance is **RESCINDED**. Isolation chambers constitute a specific environmental condition — high Compression pressure, restricted space, controlled darkness — that actively promotes Compression and Cavity Expansion developmental pathways. In 4 of 7 documented cases, specimens placed in isolation chambers committed to developmental paths during containment that they had not yet begun to exhibit before confinement.

The chamber walls themselves were the environmental pressure.

Isolation chambers are no longer authorized for developmental containment. They remain authorized for quarantine of non-developing specimens (see Section 5.1-R).

---

**ERR-03** — Issued 2088-06-03
*Affects: Section 2.5 (Acoustic Environment Protocols, Revision 2)*

Previous guidance stated: *"Maintain strict silence when operating in Stem-occupied structures. Vocal activity provides environmental stimulus that may accelerate Sensory Overgrowth."*

This guidance is **PARTIALLY RESCINDED**. The recommendation to reduce vocal activity remains valid. However, field teams following strict silence protocols have documented specimens adapting to the *absence* of sound as a distinct environmental condition. Specimens in consistently silent environments are developing enhanced vibration and pressure sensitivity at accelerated rates.

Total acoustic suppression is not equivalent to no environmental stimulus. The absence of sound is itself a stimulus.

Revised guidance in Section 2.5-R recommends low, consistent ambient sound rather than silence. Specific frequency ranges under study.

---

**ERR-04** — Issued 2089-01-09
*Affects: Section 3.6 (Thermal Reduction Protocols, Revision 3)*

Previous guidance stated: *"Sustained temperature reduction below 10°C reliably suppresses biological activity and slows developmental progression."*

This guidance is **RESCINDED FOR SPECIMENS IN JUVENILE STAGE OR LATER**. Temperature reduction constitutes the primary environmental trigger for Ossification developmental pathways. Seven documented cases of specimens entering active Ossification commitment within 72 hours of cold-chamber exposure. Four of these specimens had shown no prior Ossification indicators.

Infant-stage specimens may be cooled for transport without this risk. Post-juvenile specimens: do not apply cold.

The pattern is consistent: every major environmental intervention we have attempted has been used.

## Section 1: Identifying Developmental Stages

**1.1 Infant (Stemling)**
The only stage at which specimens are consistently non-threatening. Characteristics:
- Pale, soft, round body; oversized eyes; weak, stubby limbs
- Mass: 2–4 kg; diameter roughly comparable to a cat
- Warmth-seeking: presses against heat sources, does not pursue or retreat
- Near-identical across individuals; no visible method differentiation yet
- Does not modify habitat at this stage

**Do not assume a Stemling is safe.** It is gathering information. If it has been in a location for more than 48 hours, the chemical environment of that location may already contain developmental instructions it is recording for later use.

**1.2 Juvenile**
First differentiation appears. Body proportions shifting. Signs:
- Tissue changes along dorsal line, joints, or facial margins (method-specific, see Section 3)
- Increased purposeful exploration; systematic contact with surfaces
- Habitat modification begins: secretions, material relocation, substrate changes
- No longer identical to other specimens; individual variation now visible

Juveniles are active, curious, and increasingly capable. Do not underestimate based on size.

**1.3 Adolescent**
The Choosing occurs here. Active developmental commitment. Signs:
- Rapid structural changes within 24–72 hour windows
- Method-specific anatomy now clearly emerging
- Habitat modification at scale: tunnels, barriers, chemical deposits
- Behavioral commitment to specific environmental zones

Adolescent specimens represent the highest unpredictability. A specimen that was manageable yesterday may have committed to a new anatomy today.

**1.4 Adult (Settled Form)**
Commitment complete. Adult Sustainment Profile active. Signs:
- Stable morphology with method-specific adult anatomy
- Habitat fully modified to match specific environmental needs
- Predatory attentiveness and warmth-seeking behaviors now prominent
- Responds to environmental mismatch with visible distress; do not exploit this as weakness (see ERR precedent — the distress is also a pressure the specimen uses)

## Section 2: Building Assessment

**2.1 Signs of Active Infestation (Recent)**
- Unusual humidity gradients (specimens regulate hollow moisture)
- Warmth concentrations in unexpected locations (thermal signatures on sensors)
- Substrate modification: freshly disturbed soil, new tunnel openings, soft debris
- Chemical odor: varies by specimen method; Cavity Expansion produces sweet compounds; Connective-Tissue forms produce a faint organic-metallic scent; Sensory Overgrowth specimens near zero odor
- Acoustic anomalies: sounds that don't match any obvious source; rhythmic structural sounds through walls

**2.2 Signs of Established Presence**
- Walls with smooth, worn surfaces at specific heights — contact impressions from repeated Compression-type pressing behavior
- Sealed ventilation openings: connective-tissue or dermal material blocking ducts
- Plumbing with unusual flow characteristics: vascular material may have replaced or infiltrated pipe systems
- Calcium deposits along structural surfaces: common with Ossification-pathway specimens
- Fungal colonies integrated with discarded skin tissue: normal and consistent

**2.3 Signs of Advanced Assimilation**
At this stage, the structure is no longer a building that Stems inhabit. It is a modified organism.
- Load-bearing structures replaced with biological tissue (structural engineers required before entry)
- Temperature and humidity gradients consistent with internal body regulation, not external climate
- Air moving through the structure in patterns inconsistent with existing ventilation
- Surfaces that respond to vibration: walls that tighten around pressure
- Infants found in enclosed spaces that appear to have been designed to hold them

**Do not attempt to clear an assimilated structure without full team protocols.** An assimilated building is a single organism that includes the architecture. Moving through it is moving through a body.

**2.4 Dormant Specimen Detection**
Dormant specimens maintain sensory activity at 64% of baseline (see NEPHE-7.006). They are aware of your presence. Signs of dormancy:
- Heartbeat: 7–14 bpm (requires contact sensor; not audible)
- No movement for hours in the same position (not sleep — positioning is deliberate)
- Respiratory rate at or near zero (thermal signature still present)

Do not mistake dormancy for safety. The specimen is recording you.

## Section 3: Encounter Protocols by Lineage

**3.1 Compression-type (Popliths, Hiplurks, Elboughs)**
- Occupy confined spaces; detect pressure and heat through contact
- Do not enter tunnels, crawlspaces, or rooms with low ceilings without first checking thermal sensors
- They do not chase — they wait and compress
- Retreat along the same route you entered; specimens may have repositioned behind you
- ⚠️ Do not seal exits to trap them (ERR-02)

**3.2 Ossification-type (Patellarks, Mandigores, Shinwraiths, Heelers)**
- Hard structures; resistant to blunt impact
- Move predictably and deliberately; fast forms (Shinwraiths) are the exception — they attack first
- ⚠️ Do not use cold protocols (ERR-04)
- Shinwraiths: maintain line of sight, do not let them get behind you; they have been faster than all documented observations suggested they would be

**3.3 Sensory-type (Oculines, Laryngraves, Lobechildren)**
- Laryngraves: do not respond to sounds you hear. Not sounds of distress. Not familiar voices. Not your name.
- Oculines: synchronized blinking across colony members is a coordination signal, not display behavior; if you see multiple specimens beginning to blink in pattern, exit immediately
- Lobechildren: they are not confused or harmless; the sounds they make have worked before; that is why they make them

**3.4-R Thermal protocols (replaces ERR-01 guidance)**
Pending full review. Current recommendation: thermal neutrality. Do not heat. Do not cool (see ERR-04). Maintain ambient temperature as close to the environmental baseline as possible. The goal is to not be an environmental input.

**3.5 Cavity-type (Costal Leviathans, Palatines, Throats)**
- These forms use the inside of their bodies as the primary encounter space
- If a Palatine's sweet-smell secretion is detectable: exit the space
- Costal Leviathans in flooded spaces: assume anything that looks like submerged debris is not debris
- Throats: water showing unusual current patterns in still environments — do not enter the water

**3.6-R Cold protocols (replaces ERR-04 guidance)**
Infant specimens may be safely transported at reduced temperature. Post-juvenile: do not apply cold in any context. See ERR-04.

## Section 4: Handling Remains

The following is not an approximation or a precaution. It is documented behavior.

Dead specimens remain ecologically active for up to 60 days post-death (NEPHE-7.057). The body continues to:
- Produce chemical signaling compounds
- Release viable undifferentiated cells
- Generate new infant specimens from Day 18 through approximately Day 41

**Handling protocols:**
- Do not handle with bare skin; full protective gear required
- Do not transport remains through active settlement zones
- Do not bury — soil exposure allows chemical integration
- Incineration is the only currently authorized disposal method
- Incineration should occur at the location of death where possible; transport of remains increases exposure path

**Why fire is authorized for remains but not for living specimens:**
Remains cannot use the environmental stimulus of heat to adapt. The concern that applies to living specimens — that thermal stress becomes adaptive pressure — does not apply to dead tissue. However, teams have reported residual chemical activity in ash from incinerated specimens. Ash should be treated as biologically active material.

## Section 5: Known Failed Containment Methods

Every method below was believed effective at the time of implementation. Every method below was subsequently documented to have provided specific environmental pressure that specimens adapted to.

| Method | Intended effect | Actual outcome |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| Sustained directed heat | Deterrence | Accelerated Dermal Spread commitment (ERR-01) |
| Sealed isolation chambers | Halt development | Provided Compression/Cavity Expansion stimulus (ERR-02) |
| Total acoustic suppression | Prevent Sensory Overgrowth | Specimens adapted to silence as environment (ERR-03) |
| Sustained cold below 10°C | Suppress biological activity | Accelerated Ossification commitment (ERR-04) |
| Starvation protocols | Slow development | High-hunger conditions are primary Ossification and maturation trigger |
| Flooding containment areas | Drown or displace | Aquatic-adjacent specimens committed to flood-adapted paths within 48 hours |
| Confinement in open-air pens | Prevent habitat modification | Sky exposure and thermal variance triggered Membrane Extension in 3 of 5 pens within 14 days |

**Current recommendation:** Avoidance is more reliable than containment. The field manual will continue to be updated as new failures are documented. Submit failure reports through standard field channels.

## Section 6: Signs of Ongoing Assimilation

Assimilation is not an event. It is a process. By the time it is visible to survey teams, it has been underway for months.

**Early indicators (months 1–3):**
- Unusual distribution of infant specimens in the area — higher density than expected, concentrated near warm or populated locations
- Behavioral change in wildlife: prey species beginning to avoid specific areas not associated with previous predator activity
- Minor unexplained structural changes in older buildings: new cracks following unusual paths, materials appearing to soften at unexpected points

**Intermediate indicators (months 3–9):**
- Settlement residents reporting Stemling sightings that don't match quarantine zone boundaries
- Fungal presence across surfaces where fungal growth is not normally supported
- Plumbing anomalies: flow changes, temperature changes, pressure irregularities
- Children unable to account for where a "warm friend" they've been spending time with came from

**Advanced indicators (month 9+):**
- Chemical odor present across the settlement at low, consistent concentration — no single source
- Residents expressing unusual calm about conditions that would normally produce distress
- Structural surveys finding biological tissue where structural materials should be
- Any resident reporting that a deceased family member's voice has been heard from inside a wall

The last indicator has not been determined to be impossible.
