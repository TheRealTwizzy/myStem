# Stem Mutation Trait Codex

## Purpose

This codex is a modular mutation library for generating canon-compliant Stems across the **Stemling, juvenile, adolescent, and adult** stages.

It is built around one absolute principle:

> A mature Stem is not a fixed species form. It is the accumulated biological record of everything its environment forced it to survive.

Recognizable lineages may exist. Similar environments may produce convergent solutions. However, every juvenile, adolescent, and adult must remain individually unique.

---

## Canon Constraints

1. **All Stems begin as the same baseline Stemling.**
2. **True permanent mutation begins during the juvenile transition.**
3. **Every developed structure must originate from human anatomy or altered human biological tissue.**
4. **No mutation may rely on animal anatomy.** A Stem may glide, swim, burrow, filter-feed, or climb, but it must accomplish these functions through transformed human structures.
5. **Traits are inherited across stages.** Later growth exaggerates, redirects, supports, or compromises previous mutations.
6. **Mutations solve environmental pressures but create new biological problems.**
7. **No two developed Stems may possess the same complete mutation identity.**
8. **Perfect symmetry is uncommon.**
9. **Residual Stemling and juvenile structures should remain visible in later stages.**
10. **Undiscovered variants always exist.** This document is a generator, not a complete taxonomy.

# 1. Developmental Framework

## Stage 0 — Stemling

All baseline Stemlings share:

- Pale, soft skin
- Rounded, compact body
- Weak folded limbs
- Large forward-facing eyes
- Small mouth
- High body warmth
- Attraction to touch, pressure, rhythmic sound, and enclosed spaces
- Rapid regeneration
- Undifferentiated skeletal tissue
- Highly plastic nerves and organs
- Broad digestive tolerance
- Simple vocal mimicry
- Dormant growth nodes throughout the body
- No stable sex, reproductive role, ecological niche, or mature specialization

Small scars, differences in mass, temporary discoloration, and learned behavior may occur. These are not true developmental mutations.

## Stage 1 — Juvenile

The juvenile stage establishes the first permanent body plan.

Every juvenile should have:

- 1 dominant anatomical root
- 1 body-plan mutation
- 2–5 supporting mutations
- 1 sensory specialization
- 1 locomotion specialization
- 1 feeding adaptation
- 1 defensive or avoidance trait
- 1 behavioral fixation
- 1–3 mutation flaws
- 1 visible residual Stemling trait

## Stage 2 — Adolescent

Adolescence is a structural crisis. Earlier adaptations are exaggerated and must be compensated for.

Every adolescent should have:

- All foundational juvenile traits
- 1 major structural exaggeration
- 1–3 compensatory structures
- 1 new organ or redistributed organ system
- 1 mature hunting or feeding behavior
- 1 social or territorial behavior
- 1 environmental specialization
- At least 2 residual juvenile features
- 1–3 unstable mutations

## Stage 3 — Adult

An adult is an ecological solution rather than simply a larger adolescent.

Every adult should have:

- 1 dominant anatomical identity
- A recognizable inherited lineage
- A mature locomotion system
- A specialized feeding method
- A defense or apex-survival strategy
- A nesting or resting behavior
- A reproductive strategy
- A biological limitation
- An ecological effect
- Several residual structures
- At least one mutation flaw that was never fully corrected

# 2. Mutation Inheritance Classes

## Foundational Mutations

The earliest permanent adaptations. They must remain recognizable throughout later development.

## Supporting Mutations

Structures that make a foundational mutation functional.

## Compensatory Mutations

Structures that solve problems caused by previous adaptations.

## Residual Mutations

Earlier structures that no longer serve their original purpose but remain physically present.

## Convergent Mutations

Similar functions that arise independently through different anatomical routes.

## Redirected Mutations

Earlier traits repurposed for a new function.

## Cascading Mutations

Adaptations that trigger several necessary secondary changes.

## Failed Mutations

Incomplete, malformed, or biologically costly adaptations that persist.

## Dormant Mutations

Structures that remain inactive until a later environmental pressure activates them.

## Parasitic Mutations

Tissues, organs, or symbionts that begin as useful adaptations but consume resources from the host Stem.

## Absolute Uniqueness Rule

No two juveniles, adolescents, or adults may share the same complete combination of:

1. Anatomical root
2. Body plan
3. Limb arrangement
4. Locomotion
5. Sensory specialization
6. Feeding mechanism
7. Defensive response
8. Behavioral fixation
9. Mutation flaw
10. Environmental niche
11. Residual trait
12. Ecological effect

Stems from the same lineage should differ in at least **four major categories** and **six minor traits**.

## Similarity Without Duplication

Two Stems may both glide, but:

- One may glide using scapular sails.
- One may glide using skin between elongated fingers.
- One may use inflated lung sacs to reduce its fall.
- One may stretch abdominal fascia into a rigid plane.

Two Stems may both burrow, but:

- One may use fused palm spades.
- One may corkscrew through sediment using its pelvis.
- One may soften material chemically.
- One may expand inside cracks until the ground breaks.

The function may converge. The anatomy, behavior, cost, and ecological result must not.

# 6. Generation Procedure

1. Select one environmental pressure.
2. Select one human anatomical root.
3. Create one juvenile foundational mutation.
4. Add supporting traits required for survival.
5. Add at least one flaw created by those changes.
6. Carry all foundational and flawed traits into adolescence.
7. Exaggerate one inherited trait.
8. Add compensatory structures.
9. Add one unstable adolescent mutation.
10. Define the adult ecological niche.
11. Redirect at least one juvenile behavior into an adult survival behavior.
12. Preserve at least two visible residual structures.
13. Add one adult limitation.
14. Compare against all existing Stems.
15. Change at least four major categories if the form is too similar to another.

# 7. Canon Summary

A Stem never chooses its mature form.

Its environment applies pressure. Its unstable human-derived tissue attempts a solution. That solution creates new weaknesses, which force additional adaptations. Every stage inherits the successes, defects, scars, and unfinished structures of the stage before it.

Lineages can be recognized. Functions can converge. Names can describe dominant anatomical structures.

But no two developed Stems are ever truly the same.
