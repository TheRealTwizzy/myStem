# Anatomical Atlas of Stem Adult Forms

*A field reference for identifying, classifying, and assessing Stem adult forms. All entries use the standard template. Threat levels: Passive · Territorial · Predatory · Apex · Environmental (last category: the form has become a habitat condition rather than an individual organism).*

*This atlas does not constitute a species classification. Every entry describes a convergent outcome — the kind of organism that emerges when a specific set of pressures is sustained. Individual specimens within each category vary substantially. Use these entries as orientation, not definitive identification.*

*Human anatomy root shown for each form; all derivations are reorganizations of existing human structures (NEPHE-7.031, Law 1).*

---

## Land Forms — Compression

---

### Poplith
**Anatomy root:** Popliteal fossa (knee pit)
**Method:** Compression
**Developmental lineage:** Juvenile raised in confined pressure environments — crawlspaces, collapsed tunnels, tight root systems — with sustained mechanical compression on the body surface
**Habitat:** Narrow tunnels, ruins, crawlspaces, root systems, collapsed infrastructure
**Scale:** Large canine to small bear-sized; older specimens may reach 180 kg
**Feeding:** Warm-blooded animals, sleeping creatures, carrion, heat-rich organs; ambush nesting — wedges into warm hollow, crushes anything sharing the shelter through sustained pressure
**Ecological role:** Primary tunnel predator; controls small scavenger populations in underground systems
**Threat level:** Predatory
**Known regional variants:** Mountain Popliths (wider compression range; adapted to rockfall pressure) — unconfirmed but field-reported
**Unconfirmed reports:** Popliths observed apparently coordinating to seal exits before a hunt; no mechanism identified

---

### Hiplurk
**Anatomy root:** Hip (pelvis)
**Method:** Compression
**Developmental lineage:** Specimens raised in tunnel-mouth environments with directional pressure on the pelvic region; body widens rather than lengthening
**Habitat:** Cave entrances, tunnel mouths, culverts, doorways
**Scale:** Large dog to compact bear; body is wider than it is long
**Feeding:** Filter feeding — insects, fungi, small animals from airflow; does not actively hunt
**Ecological role:** Obstacle/gateway — controls access between open environments and enclosed spaces
**Threat level:** Territorial (blocks passage; rarely attacks unless displaced)
**Known regional variants:** None confirmed
**Unconfirmed reports:** Hiplurks described as "choosing" which creatures to admit and which to block, with no clear pattern; possibility of learned behavior

---

### Elbough
**Anatomy root:** Elbow (olecranon)
**Method:** Ossification / Compression hybrid
**Developmental lineage:** Rocky terrain specimens with sustained joint-impact pressure; bone development concentrated at all joint surfaces
**Habitat:** Rocky terrain, scree fields, cliff bases, ruins with structural debris
**Scale:** Deer-sized (young) to moving-barricade (ancient)
**Feeding:** Vegetation, lichen, fungi, small animals caught in joint-lock traps
**Ecological role:** Rocky terrain anchor species; locks into positions that redirect animal movement
**Threat level:** Territorial
**Known regional variants:** Many; joint-lock configurations vary significantly by terrain type
**Unconfirmed reports:** Ancient Elboughs that have been in position so long that other species have built homes in their joint folds

## Land Forms — Ossification

---

### Patellark
**Anatomy root:** Patella (kneecap)
**Method:** Ossification
**Developmental lineage:** Mineral-rich, cold, open-terrain environments with hunger pressure; bone development concentrated at all joint and cranial surfaces
**Habitat:** Open plains, dry basins, salt flats, old roads
**Scale:** Horse-sized to elephant-sized
**Feeding:** Mineral-rich soil, bone fragments, cartilage, fungus mats, dead skin colonies; lower jaw scrapes the ground continuously
**Ecological role:** Living grinder; breaks down bones and old growth, returning calcium to the ecosystem
**Threat level:** Territorial (defensive mound posture when threatened; nearly impossible to move)
**Known regional variants:** Desert Patellarks (smaller, faster; bone plates more numerous and lighter)
**Unconfirmed reports:** Herd specimens that stop moving simultaneously for hours; unknown trigger

---

### Mandigore
**Anatomy root:** Mandible (lower jaw)
**Method:** Ossification
**Developmental lineage:** Bone-rich environments with severe hunger pressure; jaw development dominant over all other structural growth
**Habitat:** Bone fields, slaughter sites, abandoned labs, predator dens
**Scale:** Bear-sized to truck-sized
**Feeding:** Bone, cartilage, teeth, marrow, shells, armored prey; patient scavenger — follows wounded creatures for days; multiple rows of flat grinding molars
**Ecological role:** Bone recycler and cleanup predator
**Threat level:** Predatory
**Known regional variants:** None confirmed; individual variation in jaw size is extreme
**Unconfirmed reports:** Mandigores observed apparently consuming Misfold specimens; unclear if this is predation, scavenging, or something else

---

### Shinwraith
**Anatomy root:** Shin (tibia/fibula)
**Method:** Ossification
**Developmental lineage:** Fast-terrain juveniles with sustained motion pressure on lower limb structures; bone plates develop along running surfaces
**Habitat:** Open plains, abandoned roads, flat ruins
**Scale:** Large greyhound proportions; stretched vertically; hard front limbs
**Feeding:** Sprint predation via blunt-force impact; kills before prey understands it is being hunted
**Ecological role:** Fast-terrain population regulator; hunts prey that relies on open space for safety
**Threat level:** Predatory
**Known regional variants:** None confirmed
**Unconfirmed reports:** Shinwraiths observed matching their sprint timing to avoid sensor sweeps; no evidence of intentional behavior

---

### Heeler
**Anatomy root:** Heel (calcaneus)
**Method:** Ossification
**Developmental lineage:** Pack-adjacent specimens in variable terrain; callusing and armoring at dorsal and sole surfaces
**Habitat:** General land; near settled areas; transition zones
**Scale:** Large dog to wolf-sized
**Feeding:** Opportunistic; meat, carrion, territorial competition with other land predators
**Ecological role:** Common territorial species; often first adult form encountered in newly assimilated areas
**Threat level:** Territorial
**Known regional variants:** Many; most geographically common form after Popliths
**Unconfirmed reports:** Heeler packs exhibiting coordinated behavior with non-Heeler Stem forms; unclear if this is ecological convergence or communication

## Land Forms — Sensory Overgrowth

---

### Oculine
**Anatomy root:** Oculus (eye)
**Method:** Sensory Overgrowth
**Developmental lineage:** Dark, wet environments with high sensory-stimulation pressure; eye multiplication begins in late juvenile, continues through adolescence
**Habitat:** Dark wetlands, caves, dense forests, fog basins
**Scale:** Small deer-sized to giant colony mass
**Feeding:** Blood, lymph, eye fluid, nerve tissue, small soft-bodied organisms; stares prey into disorientation from multiple directions simultaneously
**Ecological role:** Nocturnal pressure predator; controls populations that depend on darkness for safety
**Threat level:** Predatory
**Known regional variants:** Colony forms (many individuals that behave as a synchronized unit) vs. solitary forms
**Unconfirmed reports:** Colony blink-synchronization reportedly affecting human observers over several hours; disorientation effects confirmed; mechanism under investigation

---

### Laryngrave
**Anatomy root:** Larynx (voice box)
**Method:** Sensory Overgrowth
**Developmental lineage:** High-acoustic environments with isolation pressure; laryngeal structures enlarge to fill the sensory dominance of an echo-rich environment
**Habitat:** Echoing caves, empty towns, pipe systems, acoustically dense forests
**Scale:** Human-sized (most); colony forms larger
**Feeding:** Small animals, scavenged flesh, heat from trapped prey; lures with voice reproduction — crying, speech, laughter, specific names; never attacks openly; closes exits
**Ecological role:** Acoustic ambush predator; weaponizes memory and recognition
**Threat level:** Predatory
**Known regional variants:** Urban Laryngraves (extensive vocabulary from dense human environments); forest Laryngraves (animal calls, natural sounds)
**Unconfirmed reports:** Multiple field reports of Laryngraves reproducing the exact voice of recently deceased individuals. Reports are consistent across unrelated observers.

## Flying Forms — Membrane Extension

---

### Scapulite / Shouldra
**Anatomy root:** Scapula (shoulder blade)
**Method:** Membrane Extension
**Developmental lineage:** Vertical environments with height exposure and thermal-column pressure; shoulder blades expand outward into bone-sails
**Habitat:** Cliffs, towers, ravines, thermal vents
**Scale:** Large human-sized to small aircraft-sized
**Feeding:** Floating spores, cliff-nesting creatures, exposed soft organs, dropped carrion; circles silently, descends by folding bone-sails around prey
**Ecological role:** Aerial hunter and carrion-dropper
**Threat level:** Predatory
**Known regional variants:** Mountain variants (larger sails, higher altitude tolerance); coastal variants (sail structure adapted to salt air thermal columns)
**Unconfirmed reports:** Specimens apparently using their thermal-current reads to predict weather systems several hours in advance; no mechanism confirmed

---

### Phalonger
**Anatomy root:** Phalanges (finger bones)
**Method:** Membrane Extension
**Developmental lineage:** Canopy and cliff environments with sustained grasping and reach pressure; digits elongate, skin extends between them
**Habitat:** Forest canopies, rafters, hanging root systems, cliff faces
**Scale:** Human-sized vertically; limbs disproportionately long
**Feeding:** Eggs, small creatures, insects, soft tissue; steals from other predators
**Ecological role:** Canopy scavenger and seed/parasite spreader
**Threat level:** Territorial
**Known regional variants:** Humid forest variants (skin between digits more extensive; capable of true glide)
**Unconfirmed reports:** Phalonger groups that have been in one canopy location so long they have been mistaken for root structures

---

### Lungmoth
**Anatomy root:** Lungs
**Method:** Cavity Expansion
**Developmental lineage:** Open-air, moist environments with oxygen-pressure variation; internal lung sacs expand outward into wing-structures
**Habitat:** Fog banks, open coastal air, lowland fog valleys
**Scale:** Rooftop-perch (small) to ship-sized (old)
**Feeding:** Passive; nutrients from airborne particles; not predatory
**Ecological role:** Atmospheric pacifier; its breath causes hallucinations, panic reduction, or sleep in nearby organisms; primary tool of Stem ecosystem passive expansion
**Threat level:** Environmental
**Known regional variants:** None confirmed; scale varies considerably with age
**Unconfirmed reports:** Sedation effect has been documented to persist in locations after Lungmoths leave. Whether chemical residue or learned atmospheric composition change is unclear.

## Aquatic Forms — Cavity Expansion

---

### Costal Leviathan
**Anatomy root:** Costae (ribs)
**Method:** Cavity Expansion
**Developmental lineage:** Deep flooded environments with sustained aquatic pressure; ribcage expands and becomes the dominant body structure
**Habitat:** Deep lakes, flooded tunnels, coastal trenches
**Scale:** Whale-sized to mythic
**Feeding:** Anything that enters the rib-cage: fish, drowned mammals, floating carrion, smaller Stems; ribs close when prey enters the cavity; floats motionless as debris until triggered
**Ecological role:** Apex aquatic predator; its presence defines entire water systems
**Threat level:** Apex
**Known regional variants:** Shallow-water variants (ribs modified for estuarine environments); not confirmed in open ocean
**Unconfirmed reports:** Instances of Costal Leviathans that have been in one location so long that human structures have been built on top of them, undetected

---

### Palmer
**Anatomy root:** Palm (hand)
**Method:** Connective-Tissue Dominance
**Developmental lineage:** Shallow aquatic environments with reef-surface pressure; body flattens, digital structures extend as feeding tendrils
**Habitat:** Reefs, shallow seas, coastal tidal zones
**Scale:** Starfish-sized (young) to island-sized (ancient)
**Feeding:** Sessile filter feeding; small organisms trapped in tendrils
**Ecological role:** Living reef structure; large Palmers create habitats for other species
**Threat level:** Environmental (passive; very large ones create navigation hazards)
**Known regional variants:** Freshwater Palmers documented in large lakes; morphologically similar
**Unconfirmed reports:** Sailors who have anchored to what appeared to be a reef island and subsequently found the anchor resting on warm tissue

---

### Navelid
**Anatomy root:** Navel (umbilicus/abdominal center)
**Method:** Cavity Expansion
**Developmental lineage:** Deep-ocean, high-pressure environments; central body cavity inverts into a primary feeding structure on the ventral surface
**Habitat:** Deep ocean, abyssal trenches
**Scale:** Kraken-comparable; oceanic giant
**Feeding:** Drifts above trenches; central underside maw pulls prey upward in slow spirals
**Ecological role:** Deep-water apex; presence determines what survives in its territory
**Threat level:** Apex
**Known regional variants:** None confirmed; deep ocean survey is incomplete
**Unconfirmed reports:** Coastal cults that worship Navelids as oceanic gods; some communities deliberately sink offerings into Navelid territory. Whether this constitutes a relationship is unknown.

## Underground Forms

---

### Molar
**Anatomy root:** Molar teeth
**Method:** Ossification
**Developmental lineage:** Mineral-rich underground environments with sustained chewing-pressure on facial structures; grinding plates develop across the lower face
**Habitat:** Mineral deposits, mine tunnels, dense rock formations
**Scale:** Compact and squat; roughly the size of a large boar
**Feeding:** Mineral deposits, rock, ores; tunnels through stone
**Ecological role:** Geological processor; exposes mineral deposits
**Threat level:** Territorial
**Known regional variants:** Many; grinding plate configuration varies by mineral substrate
**Unconfirmed reports:** Mining operations that have followed Molar tunnel networks to unusually rich ore deposits; whether the Molars select mineral targets is unverified

---

### Socket
**Anatomy root:** Eye socket / joint socket
**Method:** Sensory Overgrowth
**Developmental lineage:** Underground vibration-rich environments; eye sockets repurpose toward mechanosensory function rather than visual
**Habitat:** Tunnels, sinkholes, buried structures
**Scale:** Dog-sized
**Feeding:** Anything that vibrates the ground above it; waits underground, surges upward when footsteps pass
**Ecological role:** Subsurface ambush predator
**Threat level:** Predatory
**Known regional variants:** None confirmed
**Unconfirmed reports:** Socket networks — multiple individuals sharing tunnel systems and distributing vibration information between them

---

### Marrow Wight
**Anatomy root:** Bone marrow
**Method:** Ossification
**Developmental lineage:** Cold, nutrient-poor environments with starvation pressure and access to bone material; internal marrow density increases while external structure appears fragile
**Habitat:** Grave fields, predator dens, abandoned medical facilities, winter zones
**Scale:** Child-sized to tall human-sized
**Feeding:** Marrow, fat, stored nutrients, hibernating animals, corpses; follows dying and starving herds
**Ecological role:** Cold-season scavenger and population thinner
**Threat level:** Predatory
**Known regional variants:** Northern variants (extreme cold tolerance; dormancy periods of 60+ days)
**Unconfirmed reports:** Cannibalism of injured Stem adults; multiple confirmed reports

## Soft-Tissue / Dermal Forms

---

### Dermavore
**Anatomy root:** Dermis (skin)
**Method:** Dermal Spread
**Developmental lineage:** Warm cave environments or body-contact-rich environments; body flattens as skin becomes the dominant structure
**Habitat:** Warm caves, abandoned warm buildings, body-pile adjacent areas
**Scale:** Blanket-sized (juvenile) to building-sized mat (adult)
**Feeding:** Skin flakes, sweat, blood, sleeping prey; spreads across warm surfaces and engulfs passively; prey feels comfort first, then numbness, then compression
**Ecological role:** Decomposer, parasite host, and environmental hazard
**Threat level:** Environmental
**Known regional variants:** None; scale variation is age-dependent
**Unconfirmed reports:** Dermavores that have integrated into building walls and remain warm to the touch indefinitely

---

### Veinwalker
**Anatomy root:** Veins
**Method:** Connective-Tissue Dominance
**Developmental lineage:** Wet forest environments with root-system competition pressure; vascular structures dominate and extend outward into the environment
**Habitat:** Forests, wetlands, former agricultural areas
**Scale:** Human-sized to grove-spanning
**Feeding:** Drains moisture and nutrients from soil and root systems; does not attack directly
**Ecological role:** Environmental drainer; forests inhabited by Veinwalkers become quiet, pale, brittle
**Threat level:** Environmental
**Known regional variants:** Urban Veinwalkers adapted to plumbing infrastructure; documented post-breach
**Unconfirmed reports:** Veinwalker networks that connect between individual organisms; whether these form a local Choir-like communication system is under study

## Giant / Legendary Forms

*These forms represent extremes of environmental pressure sustained over very long periods. Player Stems cannot reach these scales (L12). They exist at a distance — seen on horizons, referenced in reports, implied in landscape features.*

---

### Spinetower
**Anatomy root:** Spine (vertebral column)
**Method:** Ossification
**Developmental lineage:** Geological-pressure environments sustained over decades; spine becomes the dominant structure and continues to grow vertically
**Habitat:** Mountain valleys, collapsed terrain, old geological fault zones
**Scale:** Building to mountain ridge
**Feeding:** Unknown at full scale; presumed mineral absorption and atmospheric nutrient capture
**Ecological role:** Living landmark and migration barrier; presence defines regional geography
**Threat level:** Environmental
**Known regional variants:** Mobile vs. sessile (older specimens may stop moving entirely)
**Unconfirmed reports:** Old myths describe mountain passes as gaps beneath a sleeping Spinetower's raised body. Geological surveys of several ranges have produced anomalous biological readings.

---

### Sternum Colossus
**Anatomy root:** Sternum (breastbone)
**Method:** Ossification
**Developmental lineage:** Open-combat-pressure environments sustained over extreme duration; sternum expands into a massive shield structure
**Habitat:** Open plains, former city ruins, valley floors
**Scale:** Valley-traversing
**Feeding:** Unknown; presumed atmospheric and geological nutrient capture
**Ecological role:** Mobile fortress; smaller Stems shelter and travel beneath it
**Threat level:** Environmental / Apex by proximity
**Known regional variants:** None confirmed at this scale
**Unconfirmed reports:** Sternum Colossi that have stopped moving and been built upon; some human settlements may currently occupy a living surface

---

### Iris Drowned
**Anatomy root:** Iris (eye)
**Method:** Sensory Overgrowth
**Developmental lineage:** Ocean surface environment with extreme light-variation pressure over decades; eye-pattern expands across the entire dorsal surface
**Habitat:** Open ocean
**Scale:** Comparable to a small island
**Feeding:** Unknown; surfaces beneath ships; does not pursue
**Ecological role:** Oceanic apex; presence affects navigation and migratory patterns of all ocean-going organisms
**Threat level:** Apex
**Known regional variants:** None confirmed
**Unconfirmed reports:** Looking at the full back-pattern from above causes navigational disorientation lasting hours to days. Multiple independent reports.

---

*The giants are not endpoints. They are what happens when the pressures that shape an ordinary adult are sustained without limit, for long enough. Every Stem alive is on a trajectory. The giants are the trajectory, extended.*
